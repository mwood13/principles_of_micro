Chapter 5 Elasticity

5.1 Elasticity of Demand

Sometimes we want to know how responsive consumers are to changes in price. We know from the law of demand that if the price of a good increases the quantity demanded will decrease, but by how much? You might think that we can look at the slope of the demand curve. If the curve is steep then the quantity does not change much, and if the slope is flat then then quantity changes a lot. However, the slope of the demand curve depends of the units we use to measure price and quantity. A demand curve in US dollars will have a different slope than if we use Mexican pesos. The graph below shows the quantity demanded for a good in different prices. The prices in dollars and pesos are equivalent, but it is easy to see that the slopes are different.

To avoid the issue of units changing the slope, economists use a measure of responsiveness called elasticity.

5.2 The Price Elasticity of Demand

The price elasticity of demand is a units-free measure of the responsiveness of the quantity demanded of a good or service to a change in its price. The formula for price elasticity of demand is

\[\begin{align*} E_D &= \frac{\% \Delta Q_D}{\% \Delta P} \end{align*}\]

This formula says that the price elasticity of demand equals the percentage change in quantity demanded divided by the percentage change in price.  Using the graph above, we will analyze the slope and price elasticity of demand for the good in both pesos and dollars.

US Dollars

First let us pick two points. At $15 the demand is 0 units, and at $10, the demand is 5.

The slope is \(\frac{\Delta P}{\Delta Q_D} = \frac{10 - 15}{5 - 0} = \frac{-5}{5} = -1\).

The price elasticity of demand is

\[\begin{align*} E_D &= \frac{Q_2 - Q_1}{Q_{mid}}\div \frac{P_2 - P_1}{P_{mid}}\\ E_D &= \frac{5 - 0}{(5+0)/2} \div \frac{10 - 15}{(10+15)/2}\\ E_D &= \frac{5}{2.5}\div \frac{-5}{12.5}\\ E_D &= 2 \div -0.4 = -5 \end{align*}\]

Mexican Pesos

Using the same two point in pesos we have that at 270 pesos the quantity demanded is 0, and at 180 pesos the quantity demanded is 5.

The slope is \(\frac{180-270}{5-0} = \frac{-90}{5} = -18\). This slope is much larger than the slope from the US demand. If we used slope, we might think that the peso makes people more responsive to changes in price, but that does not make sense. Now let us compare the elasticities

\[\begin{align*} E_D &= \frac{5-0}{(5+0)/2)} \div \frac{180 - 270}{(180+270)/2}\\ E_D &= \frac{5}{2.5}\div \frac{-90}{225}\\ E_D &= 2 \div -0.4 = -5 \end{align*}\]

Even though their slopes are different, their elasticities are the same.

5.3 Elastic vs. Inelastic Demand

We have found that the price elasticity of demand between the price of $15 and $10 is -5, but what does this mean. First notice that the elasticity of demand is negative, just like the slope. This is because quantity demanded will always decrease in response to an increase in price. To make thing easy economists often ignore the negative sign, and just talk about the number, or take the absolute value. The price elasticity of demand can range from 0 to infinity in absolute value. The larger the number, the more elastic the demand for the good is. This means that is the percentage change in quantity is larger than the percentage change in price. If the number is less than 1, then we say that the demand is inelastic. At this point, the percentage change in quantity is smaller than the percentage change in price. If the percentage change in quantity is the same as the percentage change in price then the elasticity is 1 and we call this unit elastic.

There are two extreme cases of elasticity. First is when consumers do not respond to any change in price. This is called perfectly inelastic and has a perfectly vertical demand curve (pictured below). A common example of good with perfectly inelastic demand are life-saving medications. No matter what price people must pay, they will buy the medicine because the other option is death.

The other extreme case occurs when consumers stop buying a product if price increases at all. This is called perfectly elastic and occurs when the demand is perfectly horizontal (pictured below). A common example for a good with a near horizontal demand are staple crops such as wheat or rice.

5.3.1 Elasticity along the demand curve

For most demand curves that are downwards sloping, the price elasticity of demand is elastic when the price is high and gets more inelastic as price lowers. Move the points below to see how the price elasticity of demand changes as you move along the demand curve.

The table below summarizes the different types of price elasticity of demand.

Price Elasticity of Demand
Numerical Range Elasticity Type
\(E_D = \infty\) Perfectly Elastic
\(1<E_D<\infty\) Elastic
\(E_D = 1\) Unit Elastic
\(0<E_D<1\) Inelastic
\(E_D = 0\) Perfectly Inelastic

5.3.2 The Factors that Influence the Elasticity of Demand

1. The closeness of substitutes

Gasoline used for cars does not have substitutes. Gas powered cars need gas or they cannot operate. If the price of gasoline increases, the people with gas powered cars still need to drive places, so their demand for gasoline does not change much. Demand for gasoline is relatively inelastic.

Gas stations are all over town under many brands, Costco, shell, bp, racetrac, and more. If you normally go to a bp station for gasoline and they increase their price, you can always go to a different gas station. While gas as a category is inelastic, demand for gas stations is elastic.

2. The proportion of income spent on the good

Cheap items, like a soda or piece of fruit, take up a small portion of one’s budget. Many of us do not know the price of fruit and other items at the grocery store, much less if they have changed. Items that are a low portion of one’s budget are relatively inelastic.

Items that take up a large portion of one’s budget are more elastic. Rent is typically a significant expense. If your rent is set to go up at the end of your lease, you are very aware of this. If it raises too much, you will look for a new place to live.

**3. Time Elapsed Since Price Change*

**

When prices suddenly change people cannot always react. If gas suddenly doubled in price, people would still need to get to work and school. The demand would be inelastic. However, if gas stayed doubled in price people would make changes such as buying more fuel efficient or electric vehicles, moving closer to work, or voting for better public transportation. With these changes the quantity demanded would begin to decrease making demand more elastic as time goes by.

5.4 Total Revenue and Elasticity

The demand curve tells us the quantity demanded (purchased if available) at each price. This information allows us to also calculate total revenue as total revenue is price multiplied by the quantity. We can see how total revenue changes along the demand curve in the following graph.

Total revenue both increases and decreases along this graph. Whether or not the total revenue will increase or decrease if price goes up is determined by the elasticity.

If the demand is elastic, then a decrease in price will lead to a larger increase in quantity demanded. So even though price goes down, the quantity increases more so total revenue increases.

If the demand is inelastic, then a decrease in price will has a smaller increase in demand. So total revenue will decrease.
