Chapter 4 Supply and Equilibrium

4.1 Supply

Firms supply goods and services when they…

  • Have the resources and technology to produce them
  • Can profit from producing them
  • Plan to produce and sell them

The amount of a good or service that a firm plans to sell during a given period of time at a particular price is the quantity supplied.

The Law of Supply

“Other things remaining the same, the higher the price of a good, the greater is the quantity supplied.”

If a price is low, then firms will have a hard time covering the costs of production, so they will want to sell less, decrease the quantity Supplied. If the price is high, then it is easier for firms to cover their costs and make a profit, so they will increase the quantity supplied.

4.1.2 Changes in Supply

Just like with demand, if something other than the price affects the quantity of a good or service a firm wants to supply it moves then entire supply curve. We call this a change in supply. Interact with the graph below to see changes is supply.

There are six broad categories of things that can change supply.

1. The prices of factors of production

If the factors of production needed to make a good or service increase in price, then it is more expensive to make the good. The price of the good or service has not changes, but because it is not more expensive to produce firms will choose to make less. The increase in the price of inputs (factors of production) decrease the supply. If the factors of production became cheaper, then supply would increase.

2. The price of related goods produced.

There are also complements and substitutes in production. Production substitutes occur when two different items can be made with the same factors of production. Our tomato farm above could choose to use the same land to grow a different crop, say summer squash. If the price of summer squash increases, farmers will prefer to grow more squash. This will reduce the supply of tomatoes.

Complements of production are when a good is made through the process of producing another good. A common example is animal feed from corn or wheat. When corn is processed for humans some parts are not approved, or there are pieces that get cut off. These parts are then used for cattle feed. If the price of cattle feed increases, then the supply of corn for humans will increase

3. Expected Future Prices

Firms want to sell their goods and services at the highest possible price. If a firm expects the price in the near future to increase, they will hold onto inventory reducing today’s supply. If thy thing that prices will go down in the future, then they will offload inventory today, increasing the supply.

4. The Number of Suppliers

If there are many firms in a market, then the supply increases. If firms choose to leave the industry, then the supply decreases.

5. Technology

As technology advances, firms are able to produce more goods and services using the same amount of resources. So an increase in technology will also increase the supply.

6. The State of Nature

The state of nature includes weather, climate change, and other environmental conditions. If there is a year of good rainfall, crops produce more so the supply of crops increases. If there is a year of drought, crop production will be lower and so will the supply.

4.2 Market Equilibirum

Recall that a market is a place for buyers and sellers (demanders and suppliers) to do business.

Markets are controlled by prices. If a price is high, then the quantity supplied by firms will be large, while the quantity demanded by buyers will be small. On the other hand if a price is low, the the quantity supplies by firms will be low, while the quantity demanded by buyers will be high. If the price is set so that the quantity supplied equals the quantity demanded, then we have an equilibrium price. The quantity the is demanded and supplied, is the equilibrium quantity.

The table below gives the demand and supply schedules for a Sasha’s sourdough stall at a farmers market. The price is price per loaf and quantity is loaves sold per hour.

Market for Sasha’s Sourdough
Price Quantity Demanded Quantity Supplied
0 10 0
1 9 1
2 8 2
3 7 3
4 6 4
5 5 5
6 4 6
7 3 7
8 2 8
9 1 9
10 0 10

If Sasha sets a the price at $8, she will supply 8 loaves of sourdough. However, at a price of $8, the quantity demanded is only 2. Because the quantity supplied is greater than the quantity demanded, there is a surplus. The surplus is 8-2=6 loaves.

If instead, Sasha sets the price at $3, she will supply 3 loaves of Sourdough. But, at this price the quantity demanded is 7. Since the quantity supplied is less than the quantity demanded, there is a shortage. The shortage is 7-3 = 4 loaves.

The equilibrium price is the one that sets the quantity demanded equal to the quantity supplied. On the table, we can see that the quantity supplied and the quantity demanded are both 5 when Sasha sets a price of $5 per loaf. $5 is the equilibrium price and 5 loaves is the equilibrium quantity.
