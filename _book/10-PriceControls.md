# Price Controls

When the market equilibrium leads to a price that is considered too high or too low, the government will regulate the price. The government will either set a maximum price or a minimum price for a good or service in order to keep prices stable. Typically these price controls are implemented to correct *market failures* (when the market equilibrium is not optimal for society), to make goods affordable for households, or to keep industries stable.

##Price Ceilings

A **price ceiling** occurs when the government sets a maximum price that can be charged for a good or service. Examples include rent control, regulations on prices of emergency goods during natural disasters, and price caps on medication. For the price ceiling to be effective, it must be set lower than the current equilibrium price. If it is set above the equilibrium, then the market does not need to change it's price and can continue operating at equilibrium.
Since the price is set below the market equilibrium, the quantity demanded will increase while the quantity supplied will decrease leading to shortage. Moving the price below will show how the market changes based on the price ceiling that is set.


Because of the shortage, the market is limited by the quantity that is supplied. This lower quantity leads to a reduction of economic surplus, or deadweight loss. Because the price has been lowered, the producer and consumer surplus shift to this new price. Consumers will benefit the most from a price ceiling because they face lower prices for the same goods and services, and will capture some of the original producer surplus. Producers see the largest reduction in surplus, because they must now accept lower prices for their goods and services. The way that we find consumer and producer surplus are the same as before. ## Price Floors

**Price floors** occur when the government sets a minimum price that can be charged for a good or service. Examples of price floors include minimum wage, and minimum prices on agricultural products. For a price floor to be effective, it must be set above the market equilibrium price. If it is below, then the market is already above the government set minimum, so it does not need to react. Effective price floors encourage greater quantities supplied and lower quantities demanded, resulting in a surplus.
