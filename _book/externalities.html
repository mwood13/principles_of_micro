Principles of Microeconomics

Chapter 9 Externalities

When analyzing taxes, subsidies, price floors, and price ceilings before, we always assumed that the free market equilibrium was efficient. Or, we assumed that the equilibrium was the point where marginal social benefits equaled marginal social costs.

But, what happens when the demand curve does not accurately represent the marginal social benefit, or the supply curve does not accurately represent the marginal social cost? When this happens, the free market equilibrium is not efficient, or there is market failure. market failures can arise from a number of economic, political, and social issues. Examples of market failures are:

  • Lack of clear property rights
  • Open Access
  • Public Goods
  • Providing for the future
  • Lobbying
  • Externalities

Lack of clear property rights occurs when there is no sole owner of a good, or if there is not way to enforce the ownership. For example, things like water flowing down a river, or the air around your home cannot be owned. Sure you can own a plot of land and that land may have a river, but once the water travels down stream or the wind blows, you no longer have the same water or air as before. This weakens the incentive to keep air or water clean because do not have to deal with it permanently. Clean laws on things like water and air rights help to correct for this.

Open access goods are ones that are non-excludable, or we cannot keep people from using it. A common example of open access goods is fish. Red Snapper is a desirable fish that can be caught in the Gulf of Mexico. Because the fish sells for a decent price, fishermen are incentivized to catch lots of Red Snapper to make a profit. Anyone (with a fishing license and boat) can go out a fish for Red Snapper. Without restrictions, people would try to outdo their competition and would be incentivized to overfish the population. Due to this, the fishing industry is heavily regulated.

Public goods are also non-excludable, but are also non-rivalrous, or the use of the good by one person, does not reduce the benefit others receive. Examples of public goods are, fire departments, K-12 education from public schools, and libraries. Everyone has access to these goods. Even if people never use these goods, they are always available for use. The economic problem with public goods, is that people are not incentive to help pay for public goods, because they can use them either way. We call these people free-riders. To fix the free-rider problem the goods listed above are funded heavily through taxes. Other public goods, like PBS and Wikipedia are funded heavily through donations and often struggle.

Providing for the future leads to market failures because humans are not good at thinking long into the future. We tend to weight the importance of near choices more than choices in the distant future. When deciding how much oil to drill, or how much to reduce greenhouse gases, we are making decisions based on our preferences, knowledge, and technology today. We have no idea how future generations will view the world, or what the state of technology will be. Special interest groups use politics as a way to pass legislation that is more beneficial, regardless of if it will help the most people. The people who do this are called lobbyists.

Externalities occur when a market transaction affects people or groups who were not involved in the transaction.

##Negative Externalities

A negative externality occurs when there is a cost imposed on someone not involved in the consumption of production of the good. Common examples of negative externalities are pollution from factories and second-hand smoke from cigarette use.

9.1 Positive Externalities

A positive externality occurs when there is a benefit imposed on someone not involved in the consumption or production of the good. Common examples of positive externalities are the spillover of knowledge from education and the herd immunity that comes from vaccinations.

9.2 How Externalities Arise

Externalities occur when private and social interests do not align. The negative externality from pollution occurs because the businesses that operate the factory only take into account their private monetary costs when making production decisions, or their marginal private costs. (We will talk about how these decisions are made after midterm 2!) However the increased rate of heart and lung diseases from pollution impose costs on the individuals and the healthcare system. This is is the marginal external cost. The social cost of production is the cost to all parts of society, or the marginal social cost is the marginal private costs plus the marginal external costs.

Externalities also arise if the private and social benefits do not align. When someone gets a vaccine, they are getting the marginal private benefit of immunity to the disease. However, health people getting vaccines prevents the spread of disease to the elderly, young, and immunocompromised. This is the marginal external benefit. The sum of the marginal private benefit and the marginal external benefit gives the marginal social benefit.

We can think of the marginal social cost curve as the social supply curve, and we can think of the marginal social benefits curve as the social demand curve.

When externalities arise, we can no longer rely on the free market to be efficient. We must have intervention to change the incentives of the firms and people in the economy. Ideal intervention will push the market equilibrium to reach the point where the marginal social costs and marginal social benefits are equal. Let us revisit the factors that can lead to market failures, and discuss how government intervention can help correct them.

1. Lack of Clear Property Rights When property rights are not clearly defined, there is little incentive to take care of things. This is one of the many incentives for air and water pollution, as well as water diversion. Why does having clearly defined property rights help reduce these types of externalities?

Property rights are legally established titles to the ownership, use, and disposal of factors of production and goods and services that are enforceable in courts. A simple example of property rights is if you own a car. When you purchase a car, it is your property. No one else is allowed to drive or enter your car without your permission. If someone takes your car without permission it is a crime and they will be arrested. Because there are clear property rights, many people choose to purchase cars.

Now suppose that when you buy a car it is still yours, but if someone feels they need it more than you, they can just take it until they do not need it anymore. In other words there are not strong/clear property rights. You likely would not want to spend the thousands of dollars needed to buy a car that anyone could take at any moment. Additionally, you could always just take a car from someone else if you feel your need is greater. This would likely lead to a world where very few people purchase cars, and everyone would have to walk to work or school.

It is easy to see that clear property rights help the car market (and all other markets) to be successful.

2. Open Access Goods

Open access goods tend to be overexploited without regulation. The most common example of open access goods being overexploited is the endangerment and extinction of fish species from overfishing. The reason open access goods are exploited is because there are no restrictions on who can use them. To reduce the overexploitation of open-access goods the government can choose to either restrict access, make access inconvenient, or a combination of both. The most common methods used are:

  • Establishing property rights
  • Requiring licenses or fees for use
  • Setting limits on use

We have already discussed how property rights can be effective are reducing market failures.

Requiring a license or fee to access a good is essentially increasing the price of the good. We know from the law of demand that if the price of something increases, the quantity demanded of the good will decrease, thus reducing overexploitation.

Setting limits on use can come in a few different ways. The first is by setting certain times when the open access goods can and cannot be used. The second is by setting quotas or caps. Setting quotas limits the total amount of the good that can be used at any given time.

3. Public Goods

Because public goods are open to everyone, many people will use them without paying or supporting them. To help reduce this, many public goods are funded through taxes. One example is public education. Public education is able to remain free for all students because it is funded through taxes.

4. Externalities

There are a handful of ways to correct negative externalities, but the one we will focus on is through a tax. Recall, that a negative externality arises when the marginal private cost and marginal social cost are different. This result in too large of a quantity being produced. Back in week 4 we learned that taxes on efficient markets lead to reduced quantities causing deadweight loss.

In markets with negative externalities we can use taxes to reduce the market quantity to the socially efficient point and actually eliminate the deadweight loss. The ideal tax is called a Pigouvian tax. A Pigouvian tax happens when we set the tax rate exactly equal to the amount of the marginal external cost.

Positive externalities lead to underproduction of goods and services because the marginal private benefits and the marginal social benefits do not match. In order to incentivize increased production the government will often subsidize these industries. In week 4 we discussed how subsidies increase production and lead to overproduction when the market is efficient. When there are positive externalities, the subsidy pushes the production towards the socially optimal point to reduce deadweight loss and increase economic efficiency.
