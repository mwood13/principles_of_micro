Course Description

The objective of this course is to serve as an introduction to the basic principles of economics at the household, firm, and market level. The primary topics include rational behavior, scarcity and choice, specialization and trade, markets, and the role of government and its policies in those markets. We will address these subjects through problem solving, writing, and discussion. Simple graphical analysis and basic algebra will be used.

So how does this course fit into my plan of study, you ask? Principles of Microeconomics may be the first course of its kind for many of you. For others, exposure to economics has likely been limited to short courses in high school or a college course in Principles of Macroeconomics. For some of you, this course is also not likely to be the last course you will take in economics. It is a prerequisite for many upper-level classes you plan to take whether majoring in economics, accounting, human resource management, international studies, or business administration.

However, this is not the only valuable reason for enrolling and succeeding in Principles of Microeconomics. This course advances the fundamentals of business and individual economic decision-making. Each of us faces market decisions every day. It is valuable to go into negotiations in markets, as well as the voting booth, prepared with a clear understanding of the business strategies, government policies, and decision outcomes that will affect you.

The course will unfold with several goals in mind. A student enrolled in Principles of Microeconomics will learn problem-solving techniques for dealing with simple economic problems. Students will gain knowledge of how to express themselves using economic terminology and graphical analysis. Specifically, a student will:

  1. Understand the rational behavior principle and how it applies to decision-making.
  2. Grasp how prices are determined and demonstrate this process.
  3. Be aware of incentive structures embedded in different market structures and the resulting conduct of firms and consumers in response to those structures.
  4. Comprehend the basic roles of government in markets.

We will begin by examining how decisions are made, what factors are important to making good decisions, how choices relate to efficiency and social cooperation, and why individuals and business managers can benefit from understanding certain things about the markets in which they work. We will then explore the government’s place in these private market interactions including price controls, regulation, and select public policies. We will also connect to the international markets as we investigate the role of specialization in trade.

Assignments and Grading

Grading Policy

  • Exams 45%

  • Discussion Posts 10%
  • Economic Reasoning Assignments 15%

Exams 45%

Throughout the semester, there will be three exams: 2 midterms and 1 final. Each exam will be 15% of your final grade. Each midterm will cover the material from the weeks prior. Exact chapters will be conveyed on Canvas and in class. The final exam will be partially cumulative.

All midterms are in person, in the regular classroom, and at the regular time. The final exam follows the University’s final exam schedule and will take place on Date and Time in our normal classroom.

Exam rules and policies: Please bring your own pen or pencil and calculator to each exam. Make-up exams are only permitted for cases following the University’s guidelines (illness or family death). You must let me know before the exam date if you will miss the exam for an excused reason and must complete your make-up exam within 7 days of the original date. Missing an exam for any other reason will result in your final exam replacing the missed exam at the end of the term. You will be permitted one half of a standard-sized sheet of notebook paper on all exams. You may write whatever you like on the notebook paper. No other forms of notes will be permitted during the exam.

Writing Assignments 10%

Students will complete several short memos (approximately 500–750 words each) focused on economic writing, logical argumentation, and responsible AI use. Memos may ask students to:

  • Explain an economic concept or policy in clear, non‑technical language
  • Evaluate an economic claim or argument
  • Compare a student‑written explanation to an AI‑generated one
  • Identify logical gaps, hidden assumptions, or omitted considerations in AI outputs

AI tools are allowed and sometimes required for these assignments; however, grades are based on the quality of the student’s reasoning and writing, not the AI output itself. The lowest memo score will be dropped.

Discussion Posts 10%

Each week there will be a discussion post about materials to read. You will be expected to post your first response before class on Tuesday. After class you will respond to three other students. Your responses should be thoughtful and connect the original post to the material in class, the reading, or your own experiences. Your responses are due by Sunday at 11:59pm. Your lowest discussion posts will be dropped at the end of the semester.

Economic Reasoning Assignments 15%

At the end of class on Thursdays, I will share a more difficult practice problem. These problems will require you to reflect on the materials for the week. These problems consist of two parts: the initial attempt and the revision and reflection. The initial attempt will be 1/3 of the points. You will receive full points for submitting the attempt by Friday at Midnight.

The second portion of the assignment is based on your reflection and revision of your initial attempt. After submitting your initial attempt, the solution to the problem will be made available. You will compare your attempt with the solution. Your score on the revision will be determined by the following criteria:

  • Your ability to identify where you made mistakes or where your argument was inconsistent.
  • Explain why something was a mistake or inconsistent.
  • Explain how you determined the mistakes from above, or how you determined that you were correct/consistent.
  • State what tools or resources you used to help you determine your responses above.
  • Reflect on the process of the question, and what skills you want to try to strengthen for the next assignment.

The reflections will be due on the following Tuesday at Midnight. Your two lowest scores will be dropped.

Online Homework 10%

All homework will be done using the FlatWorld Platform. This homework is aimed at helping you further your analytic and problem-solving skills. Homework will be based on completion. Because they are completion based, you may use any resources to aid in solving including but not limited to your notes, the textbook, office hours, your friends, and the internet (including AI). Regardless of the tools used to complete your homework, I encourage you to ensure that at the end of the assignment you understand how to solve each problem. Your lowest homework grade will be dropped at the end of the semester.

Participation 10%

Participation is essential in a Socratic classroom. To earn full participation credit, students must meaningfully participate in at least 18 class meetings (75%). Participation may include discussion, small‑group work, answering questions, or contributing predictions during class. Please know that attendance alone will not receive participation credit.

Civility Statement

Oglethorpe University is a place dedicated to vigorous discussion, exchange of ideas and debate. Such discussion always runs the risk of becoming acrimonious; thus it is particularly important for all members of the Oglethorpe community to maintain a climate marked by mutual respect. We must be committed to the highest standards of civility and decency and to promoting a community where all people can work and learn together in an atmosphere free of demeaning behavior or hostility.

The need to maintain civility and mutual respect extends throughout the different venues of debate and discussion — from informal exchanges in the dining hall, the residence halls or the quad to formal discussions in classrooms, meetings, and social media.

Students and faculty have a responsibility to foster a healthy climate for the exchange of ideas in the classroom and on campus. To that end, students and faculty should specifically avoid behavior that disrupts classroom activities or creates a hostile or intimidating atmosphere.

It is my intent to create a learning environment that acknowledges and values diverse perspectives and experiences, and to select and create materials and activities in support of that goal. I welcome your feedback on this aspect of the course at any time, with any information that would make it more effective and inclusive for you.

AI Policy

AI technology is rapidly evolving and becoming more accessible to students. I cannot stop you from using AI, Chegg, Quizlet, and other platforms on your homework. However, you should remember that using such platforms to simply give you the answers to the homework impedes your ability to learn and understand the course content. It is much better to use such resources as a tool to assist in your learning of the materials so that you can show mastery of the subject during class and exams. Your future self will thank you.
