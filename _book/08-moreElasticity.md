# Other Elasticities and Economic Surplus

## Income Elasticity

Sometimes we may be interested in how changes in people's income will affect their demands for certain goods. In this case we will use **income elasticity of demand** instead of price elasticity. The formula is very similar.

$E_I = \frac{\% \Delta Q_D}{\% \Delta I}$

Where I is the income so that the formula reads, "the income elasticity of demand is equal to the percentage change in quantity divided by the percentage change in income."

Income elasticity of demand can be either positive or negative. The sign of the income elasticity let's us know if the good is normal or inferior. Recall that a **normal good** is one that consumers demand more of as their income increases, so the income elasticity will be positive.
An **inferior good** is one that consumers purchase less of as their income increases. This will result in the income elasticity being positive.

## Cross-Price Elasticity of Demand

Cross price elasticity lets us know how much two goods are related to one another. It's formula is

$E_{AB} = \frac{\%\Delta Q_A}{\% \Delta P_B}$

and says that the cross price elasticity of good A related to good B is equal tot he percentage change in quantity demanded of good A divided by the percentage change in price of good B.

Recall that **substitute goods** are replacements. If the price of Good B increases, then people will switch to purchasing A, or the quantity increases. So both term are positive resulting in a positive cross price elasticity. The larger the number, the better substitutes the goods are.

**Complements goods** are ones purchased together. If the price of good B goes up, people will want less of both good A and good B. So while the denominator is positive, the numerator is negative. This results in a negative cross price elasticity. The larger the negative number is (closer to negative infinity) the more complementary the goods are.

## Economic Surplus

Recall that a *market surplus* occurred when the quantity supplied exceeded the quantity demanded. **Economic surplus** is different in that it is the sum of total benefits received by consumers and producers in the current market.

### Consumer Surplus

When looking at the demand curve, we observe the relationship between different quantities of a good or service and the price for those quantities. We can think of these prices as people's willingness to pay for different quantities of certain goods. Say that the most you would be willing to pay for a new jacket is \$150. For you to demand the jacket you must have the means and plan to purchase this jacket. So long as the price is at or below \$150 you will demand it. If the price goes above \$150 you no longer demand the jacket because you are not willing to pay the price. For this reason we will sometimes call the demand curve the **willingness-to-pay curve**. We also assume that, since people are rational, you will not spend \$150 on a jacket if you do not value its benefits at \$150. For this reason we will also sometimes refer to the demand curve as the **marginal benefits curve**.

Now suppose you went to the store to purchase a new jacket, and the one you like is prices at \$120. You are willing to pay \$120 because it is less than the \$150 that you value it at. Because you value this jacket higher than the price you must pay, you get additional benefit called consumer surplus. **Consumer surplus** is the excess benefit of consuming a good or service beyond its market price. Your consumer surplus in this scenario is \$150 - \$120 = \$30.

Now, you are not the only person purchasing jackets. All other people you value this jacket at \$120 or more will demand this jacket. Combining all of the individual demand for this good gives us the market demand for the good. By combining the consumer surplus of all the individuals in the market, we can calculate the total consumer surplus.

When looking at demand graphically, consumer surplus is the total area between the demand curve and the market price, for the total quantity demanded. By changing the price on the graph below, you can see how the consumer surplus changes.

### Producer Surplus

Just like you have value for things you purchase, firms and producers have value for the things they sell. Firms base their decisions to produce a specific quantity at different prices based on their costs. Suppose the firm that made the jacket above faces the cost of \$50 to make your jacket. Since the market price was \$120, they were willing to produce and sell the jacket. They would actually be willing to supply jackets for any price at or above \$50, because it will cover their costs and more. However, if the price is below \$50, then they will not supply jackets to the market because they would lose money. Because of this relationship we can think of the supply curve as the minimum-supply price curve, or the marginal cost curve. **Producer surplus** occurs when a firm is able to supply an item at a price greater than their marginal cost. In this case the firm earns \$120 - \$50 = \$70 in producer surplus.

By adding all of the individual firms' supply curves, we get the market supply curve. By adding all of the individual producer surpluses at different quantities, we can the total producer surplus. Graphically, this is the area between the market price and supply curve for all quantities bought and sold in the market.

## Market Efficiency

Combining producer surplus and consumer surplus gives us economic surplus. Recall that economic efficiency occurs when we cannot make one person better off without harming another. One concern of economics is market efficiency. We want to know if there are ways to improve markets that do not negatively affect people already participating in those markets.

To analyze if markets are efficient, we can look at it in two ways. First, a market is efficient when **marginal social benefits equal marginal social costs**. I say social benefits and social costs, because sometimes there are benefits and costs of producing goods and services that affect other people, like pollution from factories. These are called externalities and will be discussed more in out study of Chapter 17. If the marginal social benefit is larger than the marginal social cost, then the market can produce more of a good and still have net positive to society. On the other hand, if the marginal social benefit is less than the marginal social costs then we can improve the net benefits to society by actually reducing production in the market. Once these social costs and benefits are the same, the market is efficient.

When this efficiency occurs, another thing is true. The** total economic surplus is maximized**. If the market produces a quantity that is too small, there is a shortage. Both sides can gain surplus by increasing the quantity. Similarly if there is too much quantity produced, there is a surplus. Firms are charging a price that consumers are not willing to pay for the quantity. This means that firms are incurring costs for products that are not sold, which reduces their surplus. Similarly, if consumers may want the good, but must pay a price that they cannot afford, this reduces the consumer surplus. Reducing the quantity in the market will increase surplus for both producers and consumers.
